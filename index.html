    Norwegian Wood

    Norwegian Wood is a 1987 realistic novel by Japanese author Haruki Murakami, the fifth full-length book by Haruki Murakami. The Philadelphia City Paper in the United States described the novel as a nostalgic tale of loss and burgeoning sexuality. The novel is told from Toru Watanabe's first-person point of view about his own university life in Tokyo in the past. The waistband cover of the original Japanese physical book reads: a masterful, poignant depiction of endless loss and regeneration, one of the most stirring 100 per cent romance novels of our time. In 2010, it was adapted into a film of the same name by director Hero Chen.

    In 1987, Toru Watanabe, the 37-year-old protagonist and narrator, arrives at Hamburg Airport in Germany by plane. Upon landing, he hears the soundtrack of the Beatles' "Norwegian Wood" playing in the background, and remembers a woman who died 18 years ago. The protagonist is entangled in a journey of self-growth between Naoko, who is emotionally unstable and mentally ill, and Midori Kobayashi, who is cheerful and lively. The story ends with Naoko committing suicide, and Watanabe starting over with Midori with a hint of sadness. There are multiple love triangles in the story, and the novel depicts the struggles of young people in their relationships, revealing a sense of confusion. Set in the late 1960s, when strikes at Japanese universities were frequent, the novel portrays the hypocrisy and weakness of the people of the time, reflecting the student agitation under the youth movement. In one passage of the novel, which describes the daily corruption within the leadership of the Japanese student movement, Haruki Murakami writes, "I think the real enemy of this group is not really the power of the state, but the lack of imagination."
